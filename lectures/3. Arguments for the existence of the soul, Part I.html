Arguments for the existence of the soul, Part I

Part 1

<What reasons do we have to believe in soul?>: How do we prove the existence of things in general? We use our senses - see, feel, hear.
<How do we prove the existence of things we don't see?>: I.e. atoms - the theory explains, and we INFER their existence. We use procedure called INFERENCE TO THE BEST EXPLANATION. Not the simplest but the best explanation.
<Example: Demon theory>: Diseases are caused by demons
-
This theory doesn't do a very good job in explaining disease
+
<Germ theory>: Germ theory does a much better (the best) job at explaining the facts about disease.
<Concept>: Are there features of humans that physicalism fails to explain, but dualism explains very well? We will run through a series of arguments. There are two approaches: ordinary familiar facts about us, and extraordinary

Part 2

<Dualist Argument 1>: Existence of the soul explains animation of the body
-
<Physicalist objection 1>: to have an animated body you need to have a functioning body. It is true that if you have a corpse you have all the parts there, but the body is not functioning anymore.

Argument 2

<Dualist Argument 2>: For the body to function purposefully you need something to be driving it (pulling the strings). Soul does that.
-
<Physicalist objection 2>: We can explain purposeful activity with presence of a part of the body that directs the body and is driving it.
+
We don't need anything as extravagant as a soul to explain how body acts and works. We must infer to the BEST explanation.
-
<Dualist response 2>: There may be a part of the body that controls it, but this part still receives instructions from something outside of the body - the soul.
-
<Physicalist objection 3>: Then it opens a possibility that humans are just robots and they do receive commands from the outside (acting out instructions)
-
The argument becomes messy! We have free will and robots don't have free will.
<Note 1>: The entire ordinary family of arguments is like that: just substitute the purposeful action of the body with any other feature (F) and you get a new argument

Argument 3

<Dualist Argument 3>: People have an ability to think, to reason, to believe and to desire. Based on these they make plans and strategies and reason about what to do. You need to appeal to a soul to explain that. No near machine could believe, desire or reason.
-
<Physicalist response 3>: Computers also make plans and strategies
+
Chess game example: the chess software is worried about having its king exposed, so it moves its queen to block player's bishop
+
We are ascribing desires to the program: "It has an ultimate desire to win the game"
-
<Dualist response 3>: We are only personifying a computer. We treat it as though it was a person. As though it has physical beliefs and desires.
-
<Physicalist response 4>: Isn't this just predjudice? Of course it is true that if we simply insist: "No physical object could really have beliefs or desires" than it will follow that when we are tempted to ascribe beliefs and desires to the chess playing computer we are falling into an illusion. But what reason is there for saying that it has no beliefs or desires? That is far from obvious.

Note 2

<Note 2>: Desires seem to be very closely tied to emotions.
+
We get worried when our chess pieces are threatened
+
We get excited when our GF/BF say they love us.
+
Our stomach sinks when we get a bad grade on the test.

Note 3

<Note 3>: Desire has two sides: behavioural and emotional
+
Machines can't have the emotional side and don't have it
+
Machines have mental life, but they don't feel anything emotionally (they can't feel, love, etc.).
