Arguments for the existence of the soul, Part III

Part 1

<Dualist argument>: We are not a purely physical system.
+
We have free will
-
There are philosophers that deny that we have free will
+
<Incompatibilist claim>: Nothing subject to determinism has free will
-
There are philosophers that believe that idea of free will is NOT incompatible with determinism
+
All purely physical systems are subject to determinism
-
The statement above is a claim about empirical science. The standard interpretation of quantum mechanics says that the fundamental laws of physics are not deterministic.
To get the conclusion, we need all free premises listed above. Each one of these premises can be plausibly challenged

Part 2

<Dualist argument 2>: In "near-death" experiences people have spiritual/religious visions of going to heaven. Their body remains on "the operating table". Something else (the soul) must have gone outside the body to have this experience.
-
<Syntactical objection>: What is going on in "near-death" experiences can not be real death, because they never really die.
-
This is a misguided objection. Even if the people didn't stay dead they may have some experience of being dead.
-
Even if the people didn't die at all, they may have something to say about what it would be like if they came close to dying.
-
<Physicalist objection>: The experience is caused by the body processes deviating heavily from their normal operation, thus changing human perception.
+
Endorphins in stress contribute to euphoria
+
Unusual stimulation of visual sections of the brain result in tunnel vision effect

Part 3

<Dualist argument 3>: We need to believe in the soul to explain a 'Séance'
-
<Magician's objection>: There are professional magicians that debunk claims of people communicating with the dead (i.e. magician Amazing Randy)

Part 4 - René Descartes story

I woke up this morning. At a certain point I look around my room and I see familiar sight of the bedroom. I move toward the bathroom, look in the mirror and I don't see anything. Normally I see my face and my torso, but now there is nothing in the mirror. In awe, I reach for my head and I don't feel anything there. I don't feel my body and I don't have any sensations.
<Descartes>: If I can tell a story where A exists and B doesn't exist, it must follow that A and B are not the same thing. And if I can imagine A without B then A and B must be logically distinc things.
+
The argument is not saying that if we can imagine something it is true! The argument is making a much more specific claim (if we can imagine one thing without the other, they must be two separate things)
